Problem & Solution

Problem Worth Solving

The EV charging industry has treated charging as a utility problem when it's actually a hospitality problem.

While billions have been invested in charger deployment, virtually no one has solved for the human experience of spending 20-40 minutes at a charging location. The result is an industry-wide experience failure that undermines EV adoption and punishes drivers who make the switch.

The Experience Gap

  • A 2025 J.D. Power study found that 73% of EV drivers rate current charging experiences as "poor" or "very poor," with lack of amenities (67%) and no weather protection (54%) as top complaints.
  • Drivers are forced to wait in their vehicles or stand exposed to weather (summer heat, winter cold, rain, wind) with nowhere comfortable to go.
  • Premium restroom facilities are virtually nonexistent at charging locations, forcing drivers to choose between subpar gas station bathrooms or nothing at all.
  • The charging experience offers no consistency. Every stop is a gamble on what amenities (if any) might be available.
  • Reliability remains poor across the industry, with chargers frequently down and no service standards drivers can depend on.

The Adoption Barrier

  • California led the nation in EV adoption with approximately 25% of new vehicle sales electric in 2024, up from just 8% in 2020.
  • Despite this growth, only 16% of U.S. adults say they're likely to purchase an EV as their next car, reflecting persistent infrastructure hesitation.
  • A 2025 survey found 57% of American drivers cite long-distance travel concerns, such as charging availability and experience quality, as a barrier to EV adoption.
  • More than 50% of EV drivers report anxiety for trips outside metro areas, where charging infrastructure quality drops even further.

The Premium Traveler Problem

Premium drivers, those who expect elevated experiences in hotels, restaurants, and retail, face a jarring disconnect when they charge their vehicles. They've invested in luxury EVs but must endure third-rate charging experiences that fall far below the standards they expect everywhere else.

This experience gap is particularly acute beyond urban cores. Scenic corridors like the Pacific Coast Highway, Blue Ridge Parkway, and Route 66 attract millions of premium travelers annually, yet charging infrastructure along these routes ranges from barely adequate to non-functional, with zero hospitality consideration.

Rangeway exists to close this gap. We're building charging locations where hospitality comes first, delivering the climate-controlled comfort, premium amenities, and service reliability that premium travelers expect and that all EV drivers deserve.

Competition

Current Alternatives

While EV adoption accelerates, today's public charging infrastructure treats charging as a utility problem, deploying chargers without considering the human experience of spending 20-40 minutes at a location. Current options fall into three main categories, all of which fail to solve for hospitality:

Mainstream Charging Networks

Examples: Tesla Supercharger, Electrify America, EVgo, ChargePoint, IONNA, bp Pulse

These networks offer fast charging at a growing number of locations and prioritize convenience and coverage over experience. Most sites place chargers in grocery store parking lots, gas stations, or municipal lots with minimal signage, shade, or seating.

The Experience Gap:

  • No guaranteed indoor comfort. Drivers wait in their vehicles or stand exposed to weather.
  • Premium restroom facilities are virtually non-existent; drivers must use whatever gas station or retail bathrooms happen to be nearby (if any).
  • Zero amenities designed for the charging experience: no lounges, no comfortable seating, no hospitality consideration.
  • Each location is a gamble on what facilities might be available.
  • Sites are transactional and forgettable, offering no reason to choose one network over another beyond charger availability.

Parking Lot Installations with Nearby Amenities

Examples: Level 2 stations at retail centers, hotel properties, municipal parking

Some chargers are co-located near restaurants or shops, which can improve the experience if those businesses are open and welcoming. However, these installations are rarely designed with EV travelers in mind.

The Experience Gap:

  • Usually Level 2 stations with slow charging speeds inadequate for travel
  • Chargers often blocked, broken, or underpowered with no reliability guarantees
  • Poor signage and discoverability
  • Drivers must leave their vehicles to seek shelter in businesses that may or may not welcome non-customers
  • Zero consistency. Every location offers a completely different experience.

Independent and Niche Charging Projects

Examples: Rivian Adventure Network, boutique solar installations, campground chargers

These efforts demonstrate that EV charging can exist in unique locations and that some brands recognize experience matters. However, coverage is extremely limited, often brand-specific, and inconsistently executed.

The Experience Gap:

  • Very limited locations, not viable for sustained travel
  • Brand-specific restrictions prevent universal access
  • Inconsistent amenity standards even within the same network
  • No guarantee of indoor comfort or premium facilities

The Core Problem Across All Alternatives

No one has solved for hospitality. Every existing option treats charging infrastructure as a utility deployment problem: place chargers, ensure they work, move on. None have reimagined charging locations as hospitality destinations where the experience matches the 20-40 minute reality of fast charging.

Site Development Process

Each location follows a rigorous development process:

  6. Construction: 6-9 months typical timeline
  7. Launch and ramp-up: Grand opening, marketing activation, operational optimization

Technology

Rangeway's technology strategy is built around four principles: performance, resilience, seamless experience, and reliability. Our charging infrastructure and backend systems are carefully selected to deliver the premium, hassle-free charging experience that defines our brand.

Charging Hardware

Each site features industry-leading DC fast charging infrastructure designed for reliability and performance. We've specified a distributed architecture featuring modular power units and satellite-style charging dispensers that avoid single points of failure.

Key specifications:

  • Up to 400kW per charging point through dynamic power management
  • Modular power electronics enabling scalability from 50kW to 600kW per power cabinet
  • Weatherproof, industrial-grade construction for reliable operation in all conditions
  • Support for both CCS1 and NACS (J3400) connectors on every charging point
  • Native Plug & Charge protocols for frictionless payment and session management

Solar and Battery Storage

Every Rangeway location incorporates canopy-mounted solar arrays paired with lithium iron phosphate (LFP) battery storage systems sized at 200-250 kWh per location.

Benefits:

  • Lower operating costs through on-site renewable generation (40-60% of consumption)
  • Peak load management reducing demand charges from utilities
  • Grid resilience with backup power during outages
  • Environmental leadership demonstrating our commitment to clean energy
  • Energy cost stability as hedge against electricity price volatility

Hybrid Power Integration

Through our partnership with HyWatts, select locations deploy modular Power-Plant-in-a-Box™ systems that combine solar, advanced batteries, and hydrogen-ready generation modules. This technology is particularly valuable for locations with limited grid capacity, ensuring consistent ultra-fast charging regardless of local utility infrastructure.

Software & Monitoring

Rangeway uses an OCPP 2.0.1-compliant charging management platform for real-time monitoring, predictive maintenance, remote diagnostics, and dynamic pricing control. Redundant connectivity ensures consistent uptime even in rural deployments.

Key capabilities:

  • Real-time charger status across all locations
  • Energy consumption and generation tracking
  • Session management and customer support integration
  • Predictive alerts from WattsUp maintenance platform
  • Dynamic pricing and load balancing
